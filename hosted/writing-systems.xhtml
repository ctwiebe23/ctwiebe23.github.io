Types of writing systems

Alphabet
Comprised of letters where each sound is represented by a single letter; every sound is represented.
Includes Latin, Greek, Cyrillic.
Abjad
Like an alphabet, but only consonants are represented.
Includes Arabic, Hebrew (sometimes).
Abugida
Like an abjad, but includes diacritics (similar to accents) on the consonants to imply vowels.
Includes Devanagari.
Syllabary
Comprised of letters that represent syllables.
Includes Kana.
Logographic
Comprised of symbols that represent entire words or morphemes; there is often (but not always) some phonemic information.
Not as difficult as they seem.
Includes Chinese.

The line between abjad and abugida is not always clear-cut — many writing systems can be both depending on what language is using them.

  • The only logographic writing system with more than ten million users is Chinese.
  • The only syllabary writing system with more than ten million users is Kana.
  • There are only four alphabetical writing systems with more than ten million users: Latin, Cyrillic, Hangul, and Greek.
  • Every other common writing system is an abjad or an abugida.

Korean

Hangul

  • Used in Korean.
  • Alphabetical, but assembled into blocks made of 1-2 consonant(s) and a vowel.
  • Only a few basic consonants (which are shaped like a mouth is when making the sounds) and vowels.
  • Other letters are formed by adding certain modifiers to the basic letters.
  • For instance:
    • ㄱ → g
    • ㅋ → k
    • ㄷ → d
    • ㅌ → t
    • 아 → ah
    • 야 → a (like day)
    • 애 → ya
    • 얘 → yay

Japanese (Katakana)

Japanese

  • Three day-to-day writing systems.
  • Hiragana and Katakana are syllabary.
  • Kanji is logographic.
  • Hiragana is the first one taught in Japan and is the default.
  • Katakana is used for emphasis (sometimes like italics) and to mark loan words.
  • Kanji is what Hiragana and Katakana evolved from, but it uses Chinese characters.
